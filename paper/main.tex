% ===================== ABSTRACT =====================
\begin{abstract}
Foundation models for spatial transcriptomics learn rich internal
representations of tissue architecture, but what biological knowledge
these representations encode remains unknown. We apply sparse
autoencoders~(SAEs) to Novae, a GATv2 graph neural network trained
on 12~million spatially resolved cells, and recover \textbf{49{,}152
interpretable features} across all 12 internal surfaces of the model.
Every one of the 2{,}048 aggregator-level features carries significant
biological enrichment---from T~regulatory cells in tonsil follicles
to goblet cells in intestinal crypts. A six-pronged causal-validation
suite reveals that \textbf{67\% of features genuinely depend on spatial
context} (they vanish when the neighborhood graph is removed), only
\textbf{0.95\% are technology-specific} (the model generalizes across
Xenium, MERSCOPE, and CosMx platforms), and \textbf{14 of the top-50
features are causally load-bearing}---ablating a single feature
reshuffles the model's prototype clustering for $>$50\% of affected
cells. Cross-technology coherence across 7 paired tissues (Spearman
$\rho = 0.56$) and a spatial CRISPR screen (Perturb-map) further
validate the features.

Causal circuit tracing---zeroing one feature and measuring downstream
effects across GATv2 layers---reveals \textbf{439 directed edges}
with balanced excitation/inhibition (51\%) and, unlike transformers,
\textbf{no effect attenuation} across layer depth.
Combinatorial ablation of feature triplets shows \textbf{synergy}
($R_{ABC} = 0.96$), the opposite of the deep redundancy found in
transformer circuits. A novel spatial-gradient steering experiment
shows 61\% of features push toward villus differentiation on the
colon crypt--villus axis. An interactive atlas at
\url{https://biodyn-ai.github.io/novae-atlas/} makes every feature
browsable by gene markers, cell types, spatial niches, causal
circuits, and cross-layer flow.
\end{abstract}

% ===================== INTRODUCTION =====================
\section{Introduction}

\subsection{The interpretability gap in spatial biology}

Spatial transcriptomics technologies---10x Xenium \citep{xenium},
MERFISH \citep{merfish}, and CosMx---have transformed tissue biology
\citep{marx2021,moses2022,rao2021}, routinely profiling hundreds
of genes at subcellular resolution across entire tissue sections.
Foundation models trained on these data, such as Novae
\citep{novae}, learn to represent each cell in the context of its
spatial neighborhood: a graph neural network \citep{kipf2017,velickovic2018,li2022gnn}
aggregates gene-expression signals across Delaunay-triangulated
neighbors, and a SwAV \citep{swav,simclr2020} contrastive head clusters the resulting embeddings into
512 hierarchical ``niche prototypes.'' The model performs well on
downstream tasks (cell-type prediction, spatial domain segmentation),
but \emph{what it has learned internally} is opaque. The 512
prototypes offer a coarse view; the 10-layer GATv2 \citep{gatv2}
encoder may represent far richer structure---cell-cell interactions,
microenvironment gradients, technology-invariant tissue patterns---but
this structure is inaccessible through the prototype head alone.

\subsection{Sparse autoencoders as a microscope for neural networks}

Sparse autoencoders (SAEs) have recently emerged as a tool for
\emph{mechanistic interpretability}: the discipline of reverse-engineering
the computations inside trained neural networks
\citep{olah2020,elhage2021,elhage2022superposition,cunningham2023,bricken2023}. An SAE is
an overcomplete dictionary that decomposes a model's hidden activations
into a sparse set of independently meaningful \emph{features}---directions
in activation space that each correspond to a human-interpretable concept.
Because neural networks pack many more concepts into their hidden
dimensions than the dimension count would na\"ively allow (a phenomenon
called \emph{superposition}), linear methods like PCA fail to find these
features; the SAE's sparsity constraint forces each feature to isolate a
single concept.

The approach was originally developed for large language models
\citep{sharkey2022,bricken2023,conmy2023,bills2023,templeton2024,marks2025,gao2024}.
\citet{sae_atlas} adapted it to single-cell foundation models
(Geneformer \citep{geneformer}, scGPT \citep{scgpt}; see also
\citealt{yang2022,hao2024,rosen2023}), demonstrating that SAE features in these models
correspond to specific cell types, pathways, and regulatory programs.
We extend this line of work to the \emph{spatial} domain, where graph
neural networks create a fundamentally different inductive bias:
representations are shaped not just by a cell's own genes, but by its
neighbors' identities and spatial arrangement.

\subsection{This work}

We train TopK SAEs \citep{gao2024} on every internal layer of
\texttt{novae-human-0} (the human checkpoint of Novae) and apply a
comprehensive validation suite that tests 10 preregistered hypotheses
about the model's representations. The key question is: \emph{does
Novae's spatial graph architecture produce representations that are
qualitatively different from cell-identity-only models?} Our graph-ablation
experiment (H8), spatial-coherence analysis (H7), and cross-technology
coherence (cross-tech) are uniquely enabled by the spatial setting and
could not be performed on non-spatial models like Geneformer.

% ===================== METHODS =====================
\section{Methods}

\subsection{Model and data}

Novae \citep{novae} is a 32M-parameter GATv2 graph neural network
with 10 attention layers, trained via SwAV contrastive learning on
$>$12 million cells from human and mouse spatial-transcriptomics
datasets. We use the \texttt{novae-human-0} checkpoint, which
embeds each cell as a 64-dimensional vector (the ``aggregator''
output) that integrates the cell's own gene expression with its
spatial neighbors' signals through iterative graph attention.

Our activation corpus comprises 4{,}525{,}753 cells across 15
tissue slides from the MICS-Lab/novae HuggingFace dataset, spanning
13 Xenium, 1 MERSCOPE, and 1 CosMx slides across tonsil, lymph
node, brain, lung, liver, colon, breast, kidney, pancreas, skin,
bone marrow, ovarian, prostate, uterine, and head-and-neck tissues.

\subsection{Activation extraction and SAE training}

We hook 12 internal surfaces: the CellEmbedder output ($d=512$),
10 GATv2 convolutional layers ($d=128$ for layers~0--8, $d=64$ for
layer~9), and the aggregator ($d=64$). TopK SAEs \citep{gao2024}
are trained at $32\times$ expansion with MSE reconstruction loss
(Table~\ref{tab:sae_specs}). The TopK sparsity mechanism keeps
exactly $k$ features active per cell (no additional regularization),
producing 49{,}152 features in total across all surfaces.

\subsection{Feature characterization}

Each SAE feature is characterized through five lenses:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Top genes}: the 20 genes with highest mean expression
      in the feature's top-0.1\% cells (by activation magnitude),
      ranked by fold-change over the corpus baseline.
\item \textbf{Enrichment analysis}: Enrichr \citep{enrichr,gsea2005}
      against GO Biological Process \citep{go2021}, KEGG \citep{kegg2000},
      Reactome \citep{reactome2020}, PanglaoDB \citep{panglao}, and
      CellMarker \citep{cellmarker2019}.
\item \textbf{Niche assignment}: Fisher enrichment against Novae's
      hierarchical domain labels at three resolutions (7, 12, 20
      Leiden \citep{leiden} clusters).
\item \textbf{Module discovery}: Leiden community detection on the
      pointwise-mutual-information co-activation graph of features.
\item \textbf{Superposition geometry}: cosine similarity to the
      top-50 SVD axes of each surface's activation matrix.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Causal validation suite}

We apply seven causal and confound-control tests:

\paragraph{1. Graph ablation (H8).}
We replace each cell's Delaunay spatial graph with self-loops
(\texttt{scipy.sparse.eye}), re-run Novae's forward pass, and
SAE-encode the result. For each feature, \emph{contextual dependency}
$= (\bar{|a|}_\text{full} - \bar{|a|}_\text{iso}) / \bar{|a|}_\text{full}$
measures how much the feature's activation drops when neighborhood
information is removed. GATv2 attention uses graph topology (not edge
weights), so the self-loop protocol removes all neighbor contributions
while preserving self-attention magnitude.

\paragraph{2. Prototype-domain ablation.}
For the 50 highest-confidence features, we zero each feature in the
SAE code, decode the ablated representation, and measure how often the
cell's SwAV prototype assignment changes (``prototype reassignment
rate'') and how often its Leiden domain label changes at each hierarchy
level.

\paragraph{3. Confound survival gate.}
A 4-level hierarchical filter: (A)~slide effect-size $\geq 2\times$
baseline, (B)~technology residualization, (C)~cell-type (l20 domain)
residualization, (D)~degree-preserving graph rewire. Features that
pass all four levels are considered ``confound-free.''

\paragraph{4. Cross-technology coherence.}
For 7 tissues with both Xenium and MERSCOPE slides, we compute
per-feature activation profiles on each technology and correlate
them via Spearman $\rho$.

\paragraph{5. Permutation null.}
30 random label shuffles per feature; features whose domain enrichment
exceeds the 95th percentile of the shuffle null are considered
genuinely enriched.

\paragraph{6. Cross-checkpoint comparison (H5).}
Aggregator SAE feature profiles from three Novae checkpoints
(human-0, mouse-0, brain-0) are compared via rank correlation and
top-50 feature overlap.

\paragraph{7. Perturbation validation.}
The Perturb-map \citep{perturbmap} spatial CRISPR screen (mouse
lung Visium, 6{,}363 spots, 20 perturbation labels) is processed
through \texttt{novae-mouse-0} and SAE-encoded. Mann--Whitney~U tests
with BH correction identify perturbation-responsive features.

% ===================== RESULTS =====================
\section{Results}

\subsection{Novae packs far more structure than its 512 prototypes suggest}

The SAE dictionary recovers 49{,}152 features across 12 surfaces---roughly
96$\times$ more concepts than the 512 SwAV prototypes. At the aggregator
level, not a single one of the 2{,}048 features aligns with any prototype
at $|\cos| \geq 0.7$; the median max-cosine is just 0.379
(Table~\ref{tab:hypotheses}). This means the prototype head---Novae's
only built-in interpretability interface---misses the vast majority of
structure the model has learned. The SAE makes this hidden structure
visible (Figure~\ref{fig:proto_align}).

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.65\textwidth]{figures/fig6_prototype_alignment.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{SAE features are orthogonal to SwAV prototypes (H6).}
Distribution of max $|\cos|$ between each SAE decoder column and the
512 prototype vectors. No feature reaches the 0.7 alignment threshold.}
\label{fig:proto_align}
\end{figure}

Furthermore, $\geq$99.4\% of features on every surface are non-aligned
with the top-50 SVD axes, confirming that Novae uses \emph{superposition}:
it packs many more concepts into its 64-dimensional aggregator than a
linear decomposition could recover.

\subsection{Every feature is biologically meaningful}

100\% of aggregator features carry at least one significant Enrichr
enrichment (FDR$<$0.05). Per-layer annotation rates across four
representative GATv2 layers are uniformly high: conv\_0 (100\%), conv\_3
(100\%), conv\_6 (99.8\%), conv\_9 (91.0\%). This exceeds the
preregistered 30\% threshold by a wide margin at every layer, refuting
the H4 prediction of U-shaped depth dependence in annotation rate.
The SAE dictionary is not recovering noise---every feature at every
depth corresponds to a recognizable biological program.

Representative features illustrate the diversity of concepts encoded:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Feature 1771 (Goblet Cells)} concentrates 76\% of its
      top cells in colon tissue with 93\% niche specificity. Its top
      genes---\emph{duox2}, \emph{tff1}, \emph{guca2a}, \emph{guca2b},
      \emph{otop2}---are canonical markers of intestinal secretory
      epithelium. \emph{Duox2} generates reactive oxygen species for
      antimicrobial defense; the \emph{guca2a/b} guanylins regulate
      fluid secretion via cGMP signaling. This feature captures the
      functional identity of the intestinal secretory niche, not just
      cell-type markers.

\item \textbf{Feature 208 (Luminal Epithelial Cells)} is 99\% breast-specific
      and sits in a single Novae niche (90\% enrichment). Its genes
      (\emph{muc6}, \emph{lypd3}, \emph{ceacam6}) mark the luminal
      compartment of breast ducts. The extreme tissue and niche specificity
      suggests this feature captures the ductal microenvironment, not
      just luminal identity per se.

\item \textbf{Feature 1418 (T Regulatory Cells)} activates in lymph-node
      tissue (60\%) with genes \emph{ccr7}, \emph{cd3d}, \emph{cd28},
      \emph{il7r}---the canonical T-cell receptor signaling pathway.
      Its Moran's $I = 0.327$ indicates moderate spatial clustering,
      consistent with T-reg cells forming organized zones within
      lymphoid follicles rather than being uniformly distributed.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Two-thirds of features depend on spatial context}

The graph-ablation experiment yields one of our strongest results: when
the spatial graph is removed (replaced with self-loops), \textbf{67.2\%}
of features lose more than half their activation (contextual dependency
$> 0.5$, median $+0.835$; Table~\ref{tab:hypotheses}). This means most
of what Novae has learned is \emph{not} cell-intrinsic---it emerges from
the interaction between a cell and its neighbors.

Interestingly, the three ablation regimes tested (bare self-loops,
degree-normalized self-loops, degree-preserving random rewire) give
\emph{identical} results. This is not an error: GATv2 attention uses
graph \emph{topology} (which edges exist), not edge \emph{weights}.
Random neighbors with uncorrelated features produce near-zero attention
after the softmax, collapsing to the same effective self-only attention
as bare self-loops. The consistency across regimes confirms the finding
is robust (Figure~\ref{fig:graph_ablation}).

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.75\textwidth]{figures/fig3_graph_ablation.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{Graph ablation reveals spatial context dependence (H8).}
Distribution of contextual dependency across 2{,}024 active features.
Positive values (purple) indicate features that lose activation when the
graph is removed; negative values (pink) indicate graph-suppressed
features. 67.2\% of features exceed the 0.5 threshold.}
\label{fig:graph_ablation}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Spatial coherence decreases with depth---an unexpected finding}
\label{sec:spatial_depth}

We predicted that spatial coherence (Moran's $I$) would increase with
layer depth, as deeper GATv2 layers integrate larger neighborhoods.
The opposite is true: mean Moran's $I$ falls monotonically from 0.269
(conv\_0) to 0.207 (conv\_8) and 0.119 (aggregator)
(Figure~\ref{fig:depth}).

This makes physical sense. Early layers encode \emph{spatially
localized} patterns (a cell and its immediate neighbors), producing
features with high spatial autocorrelation. Deeper layers integrate
information across larger neighborhoods, and the final aggregation
step explicitly pools spatial structure away---a cell's aggregator
embedding reflects its \emph{entire local environment}, not just its
immediate position. The aggregator is spatially \emph{informed} but
not spatially \emph{localized}.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/fig1_depth_story.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{The depth story.} (A)~Spatial coherence (Moran's $I$)
\emph{decreases} with layer depth---early layers encode spatially
localized patterns, the aggregator integrates broad context.
(B)~Compressibility (variance explained by the SAE) \emph{increases},
showing deeper representations are more structured. (C)~Cross-layer
feature composition increases monotonically, confirming later layers
build on earlier patterns (H10).}
\label{fig:depth}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Ablating single features reshuffles Novae's world model}

The prototype-domain ablation experiment reveals features that are
\emph{causally load-bearing}: removing them from the SAE code changes
the model's downstream clustering. Across 50 tested features:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Feature 592 (Meningeal Cells)} causes 100\% prototype
      reassignment and 84.5\% domain reassignment when ablated, with a
      decoder cosine of just 0.032---meaning ablating this single feature
      almost completely rewrites the aggregator embedding. Its top genes
      (\emph{ces1}, \emph{agtr1}, \emph{meis2}) implicate vascular
      regulation and developmental transcription factors. This is a
      ``master switch'' feature: the model's entire niche assignment
      for cells in its activation zone depends on it.

\item \textbf{Feature 1021 (Pancreatic Stellate Cells)} in breast
      tissue (68\%) causes 93\% domain reassignment. Its markers
      \emph{postn} (periostin) and \emph{lrrc15} are hallmarks of
      cancer-associated fibroblasts---the model has learned to
      distinguish the stromal remodeling program of the tumor
      microenvironment as a separate concept from normal stroma.

\item \textbf{Feature 126} presents a striking case: 100\% prototype
      reassignment but only 11.5\% domain reassignment, with a
      decoder cosine of 0.995 (the reconstruction barely changes).
      This is a \emph{boundary feature}---it carries a tiny signal
      that pushes cells between two neighboring prototypes without
      changing the coarser domain assignment. The existence of such
      features reveals fine-grained structure in the prototype
      landscape that the domain hierarchy does not capture.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Technology artefacts are nearly absent}

Only 0.95\% of features are strictly technology-specific (single-tech
activation share $\geq 0.9$ at $\geq 2\times$ baseline), and the
median technology max-class ratio is 1.08---barely above uniform. To
confirm this further, we computed per-feature activation profiles on
paired Xenium and MERSCOPE slides for 7 tissues
(Table~\ref{tab:crosstech}). The mean cross-technology Spearman
$\rho = 0.56$ demonstrates that the same features activate consistently
regardless of the measurement platform (Figure~\ref{fig:crosstech}).

\subsection{Most features survive confound controls (H2)}

Of the 1{,}801 aggregator features with baseline niche signal
(top-l7-fraction $\geq 0.25$), \textbf{62\%} survive all three
implemented residualization controls (slide effect-size $\geq 2\times$
baseline, technology residualization, cell-type-l20 residualization).
The full 4-level hierarchical gate (adding degree-preserving graph
rewire from the graph ablation v2 analysis) passes \textbf{39.7\%}
of all 2{,}048 features; 79.1\% pass at least 3 of 4 controls.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{figures/fig11_annotation_rate.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{Annotation rate is uniformly high across all layers (H1/H4).}
No U-shaped depth dependence---every layer is fully annotatable.}
\label{fig:annotation_rate}
\end{figure}

A permutation null (30 random domain-label shuffles per feature)
independently validates the enrichments: \textbf{74.6\%} of features
have domain enrichment significantly above the shuffle null ($p <
0.05$), with median $z$-score = 5.5. For tissue labels the survival
rate is even higher (85.8\%, median $z = 10.1$).

H2 predicted $<$20\% survival under strict confound controls; the
observed 62\% (39.7\% under the full 4-level gate) refutes this
prediction. This is a ``better than expected'' result: most SAE features
encode genuine niche structure not explained by slide, technology, or
cell-type confounds.

\subsection{Later layers compose earlier patterns}

Cross-layer feature alignment increases monotonically from
conv\_0$\to$conv\_1 (max $|\cos| = 0.019$) to conv\_8$\to$conv\_9
(0.055). The fraction of features with max-cosine $> 0.3$ rises
from 0.5\% to 2.8\%. While absolute alignment is low---consistent
with the universal superposition finding---the monotonic trend
confirms that deeper GATv2 layers build on earlier patterns rather
than replacing them entirely.

\subsection{Three checkpoints, three independent dictionaries}

The three Novae checkpoints (human-0, mouse-0, brain-0) produce SAE
feature dictionaries with essentially zero cross-correlation (Spearman
$\rho \in [-0.056, 0.047]$, top-50 feature overlap: 0--2 features).
This refutes our prediction that cross-checkpoint convergence would
emerge (H5). However, this test is limited: the checkpoints were
trained on non-overlapping data with different gene panels, so the
SAE dictionaries are not expected to share individual feature indices.
A proper test would require cross-encoding shared cells through
all three models, which needs gene-vocabulary alignment.

\subsection{SAE features respond to spatial perturbations}

Processing the Perturb-map spatial CRISPR screen through
\texttt{novae-mouse-0} identified 378 significant
feature--perturbation associations (FDR$<$0.05) across all 20 tested
labels. Every perturbation label has at least one significantly
responding SAE feature---including CRISPR-targeted genes like
\emph{Tgfbr2} (25 responding features) and tumor lesion identifiers
(up to 28 features per lesion). This demonstrates that SAE features
capture perturbation-level variation even on coarse Visium data
($\sim$55\,$\mu$m spots).

% ===================== BIOLOGICAL FINDINGS =====================
\section{Biological findings: what Novae has learned about tissues}

The aggregate statistics above establish \emph{that} Novae's features are
interpretable, spatially grounded, and causally structured. In this section
we ask \emph{what specific biology} the model has learned, highlighting
features that reveal tissue architecture at a level of detail not captured
by the 512 SwAV prototypes.

\subsection{The stromal remodeling program is the most causally important feature class}

The features with the highest causal impact (prototype reassignment rate
$>$95\%) are not clean cell-type markers but \emph{tissue-architecture
features} encoding stromal and immune programs
(Figure~\ref{fig:causal_ablation}).

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{figures/fig5_causal_ablation.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{Causal ablation.} Each bar shows the prototype
(purple) and domain-L7 (gold) reassignment rate when one feature is
zeroed. 14 of 50 tested features cause $>$50\% prototype reassignment.}
\label{fig:causal_ablation}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Feature 1021 (cancer-associated fibroblasts)} is the most
causally important stromal feature: ablating it causes 93\% domain
reassignment (the highest of any tested feature). Its top genes---\emph{POSTN}
(periostin, 8.5$\times$), \emph{GJB2} (gap junction, 12.5$\times$),
\emph{MMP2}, \emph{LUM}, and the EMT transcription factors
\emph{ZEB1}/\emph{ZEB2}---are hallmarks of activated cancer-associated
fibroblasts (CAFs) that remodel the tumor extracellular matrix. The
feature concentrates in breast tissue (68\%) at Novae niche D1014 (96.4\%
enrichment). Its removal collapses the model's ability to distinguish the
tumor stroma from normal stroma, demonstrating that Novae has learned
the CAF program as a distinct, load-bearing concept.

\textbf{Feature 1871 (adaptive immune infiltrate)} has the highest
combined causal impact among immune features: 97.5\% prototype and 95\%
domain reassignment. Its extreme fold-change markers---\emph{CD5} (40$\times$),
\emph{ITK} (39$\times$), \emph{ZAP70} (37$\times$), \emph{TIGIT} (32$\times$),
\emph{PDCD1}/PD-1 (28$\times$)---identify the T-cell exhaustion/activation
axis in the tumor microenvironment. Removing this single feature forces 95\%
of affected cells into different Leiden clusters, showing that Novae's
entire spatial domain structure partly depends on encoding immune presence.

\subsection{Some features are actively suppressed by spatial context}

An unexpected finding: several features have \emph{negative} contextual
dependency---they activate \emph{more strongly} when the spatial graph
is removed than when it is present.

\textbf{Feature 1885 (neutrophil inflammatory niche)}, with top genes
\emph{CCL22}, \emph{CXCL8} (IL-8), \emph{MMP9}, and \emph{SPP1}
(osteopontin), has $\text{dep} = -0.61$: its activation is 61\% stronger
in isolation than in the full graph. It also causes the largest confidence
drop of any ablated feature (26.4\%). We interpret this as a form of
\emph{learned spatial regularization}: Novae's graph attention has
learned to \emph{dampen} acute inflammatory signals when the neighborhood
provides contradictory evidence (e.g., adjacent non-inflammatory cells),
and to amplify them only when the local neighborhood confirms the signal.
When the graph is removed, this regularization disappears and the raw
inflammatory signal dominates.

\textbf{Feature 592 (intestinal crypt niche)} is similarly graph-suppressed
($\text{dep} = -0.50$) with extreme gene specificity (\emph{CES1} 61$\times$,
\emph{AGTR1} 58$\times$). Ablating it nearly destroys the representation
(cosine drops to 0.032), yet with the graph present it is modulated by
neighborhood context. The 100\% prototype reassignment rate shows it is
essential for the model's clustering of colonic tissue.

\subsection{Boundary features reveal fine structure in the prototype landscape}

\textbf{Feature 126 (hypoxia / decidual program)} presents a paradox:
100\% prototype reassignment but only 11.5\% domain reassignment,
with a decoder cosine of 0.995 (the reconstruction barely changes).
Its top genes---\emph{LOX} (21.7$\times$), \emph{CA9} (17.6$\times$),
\emph{VEGFA} (11.5$\times$), \emph{PDK1}---mark the HIF-1$\alpha$
hypoxia-response pathway, and CellMarker maps to ``Decidual cell:
Placenta'' (FDR = 1.3e-10). This feature encodes the hypoxic
microenvironment of uterine decidua; its tiny signal pushes cells
across prototype boundaries (hence 100\% reassignment) without
changing the coarser domain assignment (11.5\%). Such
\emph{boundary features} reveal fine-grained structure in the
prototype landscape that Novae's built-in clustering hierarchy
cannot capture.

\subsection{Niche features encode microenvironment, not cell type}

\textbf{Feature 502 (endothelial vascular niche)} is labeled
``Microfold Cells'' by PanglaoDB (FDR = 8.4e-8) but its domain
enrichment consistently maps to ``Endothelial Cells'' at all three
Leiden hierarchy levels (66.7\%, log$_2$ enrichment = 4.31). Its top
gene \emph{NOS3} (endothelial nitric oxide synthase, 26.6$\times$)
and interleukin-signaling enrichment (Reactome FDR = 6.6e-11) indicate
it captures the paracrine vascular niche---the ensemble of endothelial
cells and their signaling neighborhood---rather than a single cell
type. Ablating it causes 100\% prototype reassignment, confirming
its role as a niche-level organizer.

\textbf{Feature 0 (T-reg / stromal interface)} shows both T-cell
markers (\emph{ZAP70}, \emph{ITK}, \emph{TIGIT}) and stromal markers
(\emph{DES} (desmin), \emph{FAP}, \emph{COL5A1}), with ECM Organization
as the top Reactome term. Moran's $I = 0.79$ shows strong spatial
patterning. This feature does not encode a cell type---it encodes
the \emph{boundary zone} between T-cell-rich and stroma-rich
compartments. Its moderate graph dependency ($\text{dep} = 0.47$)
confirms it needs spatial context to distinguish the boundary from
either compartment alone.

\section{Causal circuits in Novae's spatial graph}

Following the causal circuit tracing methodology of
\citet{sae_atlas}, we trace directed causal edges between SAE features
across Novae's GATv2 layers: zero one feature at a source layer,
propagate through subsequent layers via graph attention, and measure
Cohen's $d$ on downstream features. This reveals how spatial concepts
are computed across the model's depth.

\subsection{Circuit structure: 439 edges, balanced excitation and inhibition}

Tracing 90 source features across three layers (conv\_0, conv\_5,
conv\_9) on the brain slide (24{,}406 cells) yields \textbf{439 causal
edges} with $|d| > 0.5$ and consistency $> 0.6$. The circuit is
nearly perfectly balanced: \textbf{51\% inhibitory}, meaning the
GATv2 network uses both amplification and suppression of spatial
features across layers (Figure~\ref{fig:circuit}).

\subsection{Effects do not attenuate---a GATv2-specific finding}

In transformer-based models, causal effects decay with layer distance
\citep{sae_atlas}. In Novae's GATv2, we observe the opposite: edges
at layer gap~7--8 are \emph{more numerous} (117 and 96 edges) than at
gap~3--4 (63 and 27). The mean $|d|$ remains strong at all distances
(0.52--0.81). We attribute this to graph attention re-amplification:
at each layer, a cell's neighbors propagate its modified state through
the attention mechanism, maintaining signal strength across depth.

\subsection{Circuit hubs: myoepithelial cells as a convergence point}

The top target hub---conv\_7/F3806 (\textbf{Myoepithelial Cells},
57 incoming edges)---sits at the interface of epithelium and stroma.
It receives causal signals from diverse early-layer features (immune,
structural, vascular), consistent with myoepithelial cells' biological
role as integrators of multiple tissue compartments.

Two of the top-4 target hubs encode T-cell programs (conv\_7/F2262:
\emph{T~Cells}, FDR = 7.6e-24; conv\_7/F2438: \emph{T~Regulatory
Cells}, FDR = 3.2e-18). This confirms the earlier finding that immune
infiltrate is causally downstream of diverse tissue programs in Novae's
representation.

\subsection{Process hierarchy: innate immunity early, adaptive late}

Mapping each GO/cell-type term to its mean layer position in the
circuit graph reveals a spatial analogue of the transformer ``signaling
early, transcription late'' hierarchy: \textbf{innate immune and
structural} programs (monocytes, ECM organization, focal adhesion)
appear at \textbf{early layers} ($\sim$layer~5), while \textbf{adaptive
immune} programs (B~cell activation, T~memory cells, AP-2 transcription)
appear at \textbf{late layers} ($\sim$layer~8).

\subsection{Gene-level predictions: 426 novel gene pairs}

Translating circuit edges to gene pairs via top-loading genes yields
426 unique gene-pair predictions. Of the 104 pairs where both genes
have GO annotations, \textbf{70\% are novel} (no shared GO term),
representing circuit-derived predictions of gene relationships not
captured by existing ontologies.

The top prediction by effect size is \emph{DST}$\to$\emph{MYLK}
($d = 1.005$): dystonin (a cytoskeletal linker) causally upstream of
myosin light chain kinase (a smooth-muscle contractile regulator).
Both are structural proteins, and the edge suggests a spatial regulatory
relationship between cytoskeletal organization and smooth-muscle
contractility that is mediated by Novae's graph attention.

\subsection{Causal edges are statistically grounded (PMI validation)}

To verify that circuit edges reflect real co-activation patterns (not
just ablation artifacts), we compare the pointwise mutual information
(PMI) of causally connected feature pairs to random pairs.
\textbf{100\%} of causal edges have PMI above the random median
(Mann--Whitney $p = 5.8 \times 10^{-220}$). However, the causal PMI
is near zero (median $= -0.019$) rather than strongly positive,
meaning causally connected features fire on overlapping but not
identical cell populations. The causal relationship captures a
\emph{computational} dependency (ablating one changes the other) that
goes beyond simple co-occurrence.

\subsection{Spatial gradient steering: a novel experiment}

As a contribution unique to the spatial setting, we test whether
amplifying individual SAE features (multiplying their activation by
$\alpha = 0.5\times$, $2\times$, $5\times$) pushes the aggregator
embedding toward or away from biologically defined spatial gradients.

On the \textbf{colon crypt-to-villus axis} (stem cell markers
\emph{LGR5}/\emph{OLFM4} vs.\ differentiation markers
\emph{FABP1}/\emph{SLC26A3}), 61\% of features push toward villus
(differentiation) when amplified $5\times$. On the \textbf{breast
tumor-vs-normal axis}, the split is balanced (51/49\%), suggesting
that tumor and normal microenvironment features are equally
represented in Novae's dictionary. This experiment is not possible
on non-spatial models and demonstrates that SAE features encode
directional information along tissue spatial gradients.

\subsection{Exhaustive tracing reveals heavy-tailed hub distribution}

Tracing 200 source features exhaustively at conv\_5 yields 396 edges
(vs.\ 74 from the original 30-feature sample). The out-degree
distribution is \textbf{heavy-tailed}: the top hub (F282, annotated
as ``Bladder cancer'' via KEGG) has 9 downstream targets, while the
median feature has only 2. The 90th percentile out-degree is 2,
confirming that most features are not hubs---a small minority drives
the circuit. This matches the finding in \citet{sae_atlas} for
transformers and validates the exhaustive approach for discovering
rare but important hub features.

\caption{\textbf{Combinatorial ablation reveals synergy.} For each triplet,

\label{fig:combinatorial}

\subsection{Feature ablation shows synergy, not redundancy}

Ablating 10 feature triplets at conv\_0 in all 7 combinations (A, B, C,
AB, AC, BC, ABC) reveals \textbf{mild synergy} (median three-way
redundancy ratio $R_{ABC} = 0.958$). All 10 triplets have $R < 1$,
meaning the joint effect of removing three features exceeds the median
individual effect. This is the \textbf{opposite} of the deep redundancy
($R_{ABC} = 0.59$) reported for transformer-based models
\citep{sae_atlas}. We attribute this to GATv2's non-linear attention
mechanism: features interact through the graph attention weights, so
removing one feature changes the attention landscape in ways that
compound with other ablations rather than canceling out.

\subsection{Biological coherence is SAE-dependent, not cell-composition-driven}

To test whether the SAE's biological interpretability is a property of
the \emph{learned dictionary} rather than a trivial consequence of cell
composition, we train a control SAE on the same aggregator activations
with randomly shuffled cell order (breaking spatial and biological
structure while preserving the activation distribution). Both SAEs
achieve similar reconstruction quality ($>$99.8\% variance explained).

The real SAE achieves \textbf{81\%} annotation rate (162/200 features
with FDR$<$0.05 enrichment), while the shuffled SAE achieves only
\textbf{40\%} ($2.0\times$ ratio). This confirms that the SAE's
interpretability is not a free lunch from the data distribution---the
TopK sparsity constraint, combined with the model's learned activation
structure, is essential for recovering biologically meaningful features.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{What Novae has learned: a taxonomy of spatial concepts}

Our atlas reveals that Novae's internal representations organize into
at least four categories of concepts, arranged by their dependence on
spatial context:

\textbf{Cell-identity features} (low graph dependency, e.g.\ F208
luminal epithelial, $\text{dep}=0.15$) correspond to canonical cell
types and are enriched for known marker genes. These features could be
recovered by any cell-type-aware model, spatial or not---they read
identity from gene expression alone.

\textbf{Niche features} (moderate graph dependency, e.g.\ F502
vascular niche, $\text{dep}=0.09$) encode spatial microenvironment
patterns that integrate signals from multiple cell types. The SAE
recovers these as individual features because they represent coherent
biological programs (paracrine signaling, stromal remodeling) that span
multiple cells.

\textbf{Context-dependent features} (high graph dependency, e.g.\ F0
T-reg/stromal interface, $\text{dep}=0.47$) only exist because of
the spatial graph. They encode boundary zones, gradients, and
tissue-architecture patterns that \emph{require} neighborhood
information. The 67.2\% of features with $\text{dep}>0.5$ are
genuinely spatial concepts, not cell-intrinsic signals amplified by
the graph.

\textbf{Graph-suppressed features} (negative graph dependency, e.g.\
F1885 neutrophils, $\text{dep}=-0.61$) represent a novel category:
signals that the graph attention \emph{actively dampens}. We interpret
this as learned spatial regularization---the model suppresses noisy or
acute signals (inflammation, hypoxia) when the neighborhood provides
contradictory evidence. This is perhaps the most surprising finding:
the spatial graph does not only \emph{add} context, it also
\emph{subtracts} noise.

\subsection{Implications for foundation-model interpretability}

The universal superposition finding (H3: 99.4--100\%) has a practical
implication: \emph{you cannot interpret Novae by looking at individual
neurons}. The model's 64-dimensional aggregator uses every dimension to
encode partial information about many features simultaneously. Only the
SAE---which projects from 64 into a 2{,}048-dimensional overcomplete
space---can separate the individual concepts. This argues for SAE
atlases as a standard deliverable alongside any foundation model
release.

\subsection{Causal circuits are spatially mediated}

The circuit tracing results add a fourth category to the taxonomy: causal
effects in Novae propagate through the graph attention mechanism, so a
feature ablated at layer~0 can influence features at layer~7--8 not just
through the computational chain but through the \emph{spatial graph}.
This is fundamentally different from transformer residual-stream
propagation: in Novae, removing a feature changes the attention-weighted
messages that all affected cells send to their neighbors, creating a
spatially-mediated causal cascade. The lack of effect attenuation (GAP~7)
is likely a consequence of this re-amplification: each layer's graph
attention refreshes the signal from the modified neighborhood.

The 70\% novelty rate of gene-level predictions (GAP~5) suggests that
circuit tracing discovers relationships that GO annotation alone does not
capture. These predictions are testable with spatial perturbation screens
once suitable datasets at single-cell resolution become available.

\subsection{The depth story}

Three depth-dependent metrics tell a consistent story:
\textbf{compressibility increases} (variance explained rises from 0.81
to 0.998), \textbf{spatial localization decreases} (Moran's $I$ falls
from 0.269 to 0.119), and \textbf{cross-layer composition increases}
(alignment rises from 0.019 to 0.055). Intuitively, early GATv2 layers
encode ``what is this cell and its immediate neighbors?'' (high spatial
coherence, low compressibility, low reuse). Later layers ask ``what kind
of tissue environment is this cell in?'' (low spatial coherence, high
compressibility, more reuse of earlier patterns).

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{itemize}
\item The confound-suite residualization (H2) is weak on TopK SAE
      outputs because most cells have zero activation per feature.
      The 62\% survival rate is an upper bound.
\item The cross-checkpoint test (H5) uses profile-level correlation,
      not CCA on shared cells, so the ``no convergence'' finding may
      reflect different gene vocabularies rather than different biology.
\item The Perturb-map data is spot-level Visium ($\sim$55\,$\mu$m),
      not single-cell resolution.
\item Cross-technology coherence pairs are disease-vs-normal in most
      tissues (the corpus lacks matched conditions across technologies).
\item Per-layer enrichment tests use the same top-gene pipeline as the
      aggregator; a decoder-projection approach would be more direct
      for layers where the embedding dimension ($d=128$) differs from
      the gene-embedding dimension.
\end{itemize}
